Overview

Circular economy (CE) aims to decouple economic growth from resource consumption by cycling products and materials back into production, either by returning materials to generate new products, or by releasing benign substances to the environment through degradation. A truly circular economy keeps material in continuous use by design. The overall adoption of CE principles has been incremental at best – especially in the US. To achieve – or even begin to achieve – a circular economy, a convergent research approach needs to be employed as a multitude of disciplines -- from material science, chemistry, biology, engineering, business, economics, social sciences, and behavioral sciences need to work in concert for circularity. Thus, we are inviting you to be a part of this journey. By deeply integrating these diverse disciplines, we can begin to work on tackling the complex challenges that currently inhibit the growth of the circular economy today.

The US lags far behind in CE design research and policy formulation in comparison with our UK, EU, and Asian colleagues, and our plan is to include a retrospective/lessons learned session from policy makers to inform the researcher community. A key outcome would be to learn where current policies create barriers to wider implementation of circular concepts. It is noteworthy that the emerging CE-based studies resemble many of the same conversations that occurred in the biomass conversion community starting in the early 2000s. Given the successes and failures of the biofuels and bioproducts community, we propose to include some aspects of learning from this community related to scale-up, development of rigorous standards and protocols to enable comparisons of various design and reuse strategies, and the need for early and comprehensive analyses for sustainability and supply chains.

Who Should Register to Attend

Our stakeholder community includes the research/academic community, government, industry, non-profits, foundations, and international partners; this list is not inclusive but will be further developed as the workshop nears. We have communicated with several key personnel from the National Science Foundation, including Linda Molnar to obtain strategic and tactical information on the Convergence Accelerator program, Keith Chanon to garner his international expertise, Bruce Hamilton for his expertise on sustainable engineering and programmatic questions, and Christy Payne for her extensive work with plastics and the recent EFRI efforts. We have also discussed this with colleagues from the US Department of Energy, Energy Efficiency and Renewable Energy (EERE), including Dr. Nichole Fitzgerald, who manages the DOE-funded BOTTLE Consortium. She has noted that DOE could bring further support to, and stakeholder engagement in, the proposed workshop.

We will work to strike a balance between breadth and depth, drawing on recommendations from the Planning Committee. A key point of the workshop is to understand how all of these group work together and eventually converge on solutions.

  • Academic community: Engineering (civil and environmental, chemical, materials, manufacturing), chemistry, biology, social and behavioral sciences, economics, ecology and business.
  • Professional societies: International Society for Industrial Ecology, professional engineering societies such as the American Institute of Chemical Engineers, Solid Waste Association of North America, American Chemical Society Green Chemistry Institute
  • Non-profits and Foundationsz: Rocky Mountain Institute, National Council for Science and the Environment, Ellen MacArthur Foundation, International Living Future Institute, Ocean Conservancy, Environmental Defense Fund, National Geographic Society, World Wildlife Fund, Environmental Research and Education Foundation, Pew Foundation
  • International considerations: Asia Pacific Economic Cooperation (APEC), Trans-Atlantic Plastics Working Group, Association of Southeast Asian Nations (ASEAN), Partnerships in Environmental Management for the Seas of East Asia (PEMSEA), G7, G20, United Nations (UNEA, GPA/GPML), the World Bank, Inter-American Development Bank
  • Governmental entities: NSF, Department of Energy EERE and Office of Science, BOTTLE consortium, NIST, U.S. State Department, US EPA Asia Pacific Economic Cooperation (APEC)
  • Industry stakeholders: Consortium groups: Alliance to End Plastic Waste, Plastics Europe, Chamber of Commerce, Bioplastic Feedstock Alliance
  • Individual companies: Amazon, Walmart, Mars, Dell, KraftHeinz, Shell, ExxonMobil, Dow, Covestro, Shaw Industries, Interface, BASF, Waste Management
  • Investment and Incubation: Circulate Capital, The Circulate Initiative, Encourage Capital

Irrespective of expertise, we are most interested in new, innovative ideas and original thinking that arise from new collaborations between people of diverse backgrounds and varying expertise.

All participants should be willing to engage in frank disclosure and assessment of ideas in a collegial and professional fashion. To facilitate open sharing, all meeting discussions will be considered a private communication and not to be shared outside of the meeting, unless approved by the contributor. All research-related application information will be kept confidential.
