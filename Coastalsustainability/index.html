Overview

Human communities and economies are concentrated along the world’s coastlines. Yet ongoing changes in climate continue to drive complex interactions between geophysical and social-ecological systems that amplify environmental threats facing coastal communities and their economies. Such amplification also magnifies existing inequities in the hidden coast – non-oceanfront regions where rural, minority, and/or impoverished communities reside. Primary threats to coastal communities include inundation of coastal infrastructure by sea level rise and coastal storms, deteriorating coastal water quality, overexploitation of fisheries and increasing food insecurity, and past sustainability solutions with unintended consequences including exacerbation of social inequity and environmental injustice. The Coastal Sustainability workshop leverages nature-based solutions as a framework to explore and shape the co-development of convergent research that is stakeholder-driven, inclusive, and focused on implementation of sustainability solutions that promote clean, safe, smart, and equitable coastal communities.

The Challenge

Sustaining human coastal communities is a urgent societal priority. While this urgency is driven by complex challenges, it also opens new doors of opportunity to (1) move away from entrenched and outdated perspective on the relationship between people and nature and (2) co-develop solutions that are environmentally sound and socially equitable. Coastal Sustainability workshop participants will meet these challenges by embracing convergent research that integrates the expertise of geophysical, biological, cognitive and social scientists, and engineers, and fully co-develops strategies with diverse stakeholders and decision makers. The nature-based framework of the workshop will focus on four vertical (Aquaculture, Watershed Planning, Living Shorelines, Managed Retreat) and four horizontal (Stakeholder-Participation, Social & Cognitive Drivers, Technology, Finance) themes that create “nodes of opportunity” that sit within a landscape of uncertainty: in management practice, in scientific and societal understanding, and in cognition of threats and opportunities. Determining the basis of these uncertainties and overcoming it will be fundamentally important to identifying and optimizing sustainability solutions and their viability for implementation.

A convergent approach is needed to overcome these challenges and make significant intellectual and societal advances. Such an approach could leverage existing programs that fund research related to Coastlines and its consequences. Through developing a coordinated effort, significant advances can be made. Collaborations between researchers in diverse biological fields and quantitative scientists (e.g. mathematicians, computer scientists, Earth scientists, and engineers) with relevant expertise could lead to better approaches to interrogate and predict wildfire behavior and ecological consequences using available data, in situ and remote sensing, and new experimental systems.

Both the Coastal Sustainability Institute and KI are dedicated to ensuring that broad constituencies have a voice at our workshop, with particular emphasis on stakeholders from systemically marginalized groups. Historically, efforts to promote coastal sustainability through nature-based solutions have primarily included individuals with privileged opportunity and educational background, with significant capital investment in businesses or property vulnerable to climate change, and/or with occupational incentives for dedicating their time (Jurjonas et al. 2020). We will actively recruit individuals who work closely with stakeholders from systemically marginalized communities, including municipal individuals responsible for housing, water, waste management, and economic development (Shi et al. 2016). We will also work closely with community and social justice advocacy groups, and we will provide participant support costs to facilitate their participation.

Meeting Outcomes

This convergence accelerator workshop will focus on fundamental and translational science and engineering that collaborates with stakeholders to co-develop knowledge, solutions and tools. Recognizing that both scientific and societal barriers prevent their full realization, the workshop will leverage nature-based solutions as an overarching framework that seeks to promote convergence across four vertical and four horizontal themes (Figure 1). The verticals represent categories of nature-based solutions and the horizontals represent cross-cutting approaches needed to fully implement the verticals. The intersection of each of these horizontal and vertical themes – the nodes – represent pockets of opportunity amidst uncertainty in management approaches, the science driving them, and the stakeholder communities that will ultimately be impacted by potential sustainability solutions. We will do a deep dive with workshop participants to identify scientific priorities that include reversing and preventing the causes of impaired water quality (e.g., harmful algal blooms and hypoxia) and improving coastal infrastructure including new approaches to cope with threats posed by sea level rise and coastal storm surge. We also will focus on barriers to full realization by integrating the cognitive and social sciences to determine how human attitudes, beliefs, and preferences dictate the viability of sustainability solutions. A key point of emphasis will be promotion of equitable outcomes while optimizing the various trade-offs that exist between economic, social-ecological, geophysical drivers and their interaction with human attitudes, beliefs and preferences.
