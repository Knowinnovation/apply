Space Materials Workshop

July 24, 2023 - July 27, 2023

Overview

Given the increasing military, scientific, and commercial use of Earth orbital and cislunar space, as well as the inexorable desire of humans to explore farther regions of the solar system, the need for new materials that can withstand harsh space environments will continue unabated. However, the more distant destinations mean that our ability to be confident in decisions based on flight heritage data or to conduct flight testing of candidate spacecraft materials will become more limited. Thus, high-fidelity ground-based experimental and modeling efforts that can be used to guide materials design and predict performance will become ever more valuable. With numerous roadmaps to space and decadal surveys being proposed, little attention is being paid to the materials that will be required to function in relevant space environments. We must ensure that materials technology can meet future needs. A virtual workshop will gather experts from materials science, space environments effects, spacecraft engineering, and mission planning communities to identify current and future needs for materials that can perform their functions in various space environments and define materials research areas where investment is likely to have the greatest impact on future missions.

Challenge

The study of materials science, processing, and manufacturing has received a huge amount of attention and has been the subject of two recent workshops. On the other hand, materials research aimed at the development of space-durable materials in harsh space environments has languished. Given the increasing military, scientific, and commercial use of Earth orbital and cislunar space, as well as the inexorable desire of humans to explore regions of the solar system beyond Earth, the need for new materials that can withstand harsh space environments will continue unabated. However, the more distant destinations mean that our ability to be confident in decisions based on flight heritage data or to conduct flight testing of candidate spacecraft materials will become more limited. Thus, high-fidelity ground-based experimental and modeling efforts that can be used to guide materials design and predict performance will become ever more valuable. Not since the workshops that followed the return of Long Duration Exposure Facility 30 years ago have researchers from disparate materials science and space environmental effects communities attempted to identify areas of critical materials needs for future missions. With numerous roadmaps to space and decadal surveys being proposed, little attention is being paid to the materials that will be required to function in whatever environment might be relevant. Commensurate with our ability to dream up a future of human expansion into space, we must ensure that materials technology can meet future needs.

Space Environments Focus Areas (Materials in...):

  • Vacuum/Low Pressure (outgassing, contamination, orbital drag)
  • Atomic Oxygen (etching, oxidation, spallation, bleaching, altered mechanical and electromagnetic properties)
  • Ultraviolet Radiation (outgassing, altered optical, mechanical, and electromagnetic properties)
  • Particulate or Ionizing Radiation (single-event upsets, bit errors, and latchups, altered optical, mechanical, and electromagnetic properties)
  • Plasma (charge buildup which can lead to ion sputtering, arcing, and parasitic currents in solar arrays, as well as reattraction of contamination)
  • Temperature Extremes and Thermal Cycling (Coefficients of Thermal Expansion [CTE] Mismatch) (temperature extremes beyond limit for material function or for handling, mechanical failure)
  • Micrometeoroid/Orbital Debris Impact (physical damage, degradation of various properties plus release of contamination)
  • Lunar/Planetary Dust (adhesion, abrasion, surface electric fields, degradation of optical properties)

Event Outcomes

Key Outcomes Include:

  • Summary report on workshop topics to identify prioritized areas for materials synthesis, manufacturing, and testing.
  • Educational tools for non-experts in the field.
  • Education of early career scientists
